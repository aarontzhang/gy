# Problem Spaces

Forward-looking spaces worth building in. Not ideas — spaces. Each contains many possible wedges.

---

## 1. The Solo Operator Economy

**Thesis:** The rise of individuals running $1M–$50M+ businesses alone. Not solopreneur gig workers — real companies that used to require 20-person teams. One person running 10 brands, 3 content channels, and 50 AI agents. Every layer of existing business software — HR, ops, finance, collaboration — assumes a team. That assumption is about to be wrong for an entire class of businesses.

**Why now:** AI can finally do the "coworker" functions at real quality: customer support, content ops, bookkeeping, data analysis, creative production, project management, sourcing, research, outbound. A single operator with the right stack now leverages 50–100 agents doing specialized work. This was structurally impossible 2 years ago.

**Customer archetypes:**
- Creator-turned-operator (YouTube → DTC brand → info products → software)
- E-commerce portfolio owner running 5–20 Shopify / Amazon stores
- Solo SaaS founder at $1–$50M ARR running everything themselves
- Freelancer scaling into a firm-of-one (consulting, design, writing, video)
- Rollup operators acquiring and running multiple SMBs with AI-native back offices
- "Lifestyle empire" operators: one person, multiple cashflow streams, no employees

**Possible wedges (non-exhaustive):**
- **Financial back office for solo operators** — bookkeeping across multiple entities, tax, multi-state compliance, revenue recognition, personal + business blending.
- **Agent HR** — a roster / performance / swap layer for the AI agents doing your work. Cross-model, cross-provider, with logs, audits, and A/B comparison of agents for a given job.
- **Ops command center** — one pane of glass (voice-native) for running 10 businesses. Not a dashboard in the 2015 sense — a conversational operating layer.
- **Insurance / legal / benefits** designed for one-person entities that behave like 20-person companies.
- **M&A infrastructure for solo operators** — financing, diligence, and buyer–seller matching for people acquiring or flipping small online businesses.
- **Rollup financials** — real-time P&L rollup across dozens of small businesses owned by one operator, with automated inter-entity bookkeeping.
- **Hiring-the-first-human** — the step from 1 to 2 is brutal and poorly served; specific tooling for first-hire compliance, payroll, equity, and onboarding.

**Why most VCs miss it:** "SMB" to VCs means a 10–500 person company. The unit that matters now is 1. They're still designing software for a world that has fewer and fewer of those.

**Risks:**
- Solo operators are historically cheap (low WTP per seat).
- Churn is high because switching costs are low without a team enforcing status quo.
- Mitigation: price on outcomes or revenue share, not seats. Or price on the things solo operators actually spend real money on (bookkeepers, lawyers, insurance) — not the things they'd ideally want for $20/month.

## 2. The AI-Native Professional Services Firm

**Thesis:** Don't sell tools to existing firms. Build the firm. An AI-native law firm, accounting firm, marketing agency, design studio, research practice, M&A shop — staffed with 3–5 senior humans each running 50+ agents. Deliver the output of a 50-person firm at 1/3 the price with 10x the margin. This is the "swallow the company" thesis scaled to professional services.

**Why now:** AI is genuinely capable of 80%+ of the drafting, research, analysis, and documentation work in most knowledge-services industries. Incumbents are structurally incapable of adopting it because their business model depends on billable hours — cannibalizing themselves means collapsing their revenue. A green-field competitor has no such constraint.

**Industries ranked by fit** (low regulation, fast cash cycle, crisp deliverables, customers actively unhappy with incumbents):

1. **Marketing / creative agencies** — zero regulation, fast cash, output-focused. AI already does 60%+ of the creative work. Huge TAM, huge incumbent dissatisfaction, notoriously high agency markup.
2. **Architectural & interior design services** — some licensing (full architects) but large segments are unlicensed (commercial fit-out, space planning, staging, interior design, retail design). Design-taste-driven, deliverables are crisp.
3. **Technical writing & documentation** — B2B, boring, huge market, incumbent firms are behind on AI. Software companies spend massive budgets here.
4. **Translation & localization** — exploding because of global content, AI is production-ready, incumbents are price-competing themselves out.
5. **Market research firms** — being displaced anyway. Offer the AI-native version that delivers in 48 hours for 1/5 the price of a McKinsey-tier study.
6. **Patent drafting support (not filing)** — large portion is boilerplate. Work with USPTO attorneys on the back end; productize the drafting.
7. **Small-cap M&A advisory** — document-heavy, paper-heavy, fee-heavy. Underserved segment between DIY and big-bank IB.
8. **PR / communications agencies** — commodity work, AI-capable, fat agency markup.
9. **Bookkeeping** (not CPA-tier) — transaction categorization, close process, reconciliation, AR/AP. AI-native here is massive.
10. **Insurance broking for SMB** — middleman margin is fat, comparison is mechanical.
11. **Executive search at junior–mid levels** — sourcing + screening, huge margins (30% placement fees), incumbents are relationship businesses poorly equipped for AI.
12. **Mid-market strategy / ops consulting** — McKinsey sells $5M engagements the mid-market can't afford. An AI-native firm can deliver the same output at $100K.
13. **Paralegal / legal ops services** — contract review, compliance, intake. Everything non-licensed that law firms mark up 10x.
14. **Bookkeeping + tax prep for creators and online businesses** — specific sub-vertical; these customers have money and deeply hate their current solutions.
15. **Brand / identity design studios** — logo, brand guidelines, naming. AI output is already at parity with midmarket studios; incumbent fees are 5–20x cost.

**Selection criteria for which category to pick first:**
1. AI output quality is already at or above midmarket human parity for the main deliverables.
2. Licensing / regulatory bar is minimal or navigable with a licensed partner on the back end.
3. Customers are actively unhappy with incumbents, or actively priced out of them.
4. Deliverables are crisp (document, design, spec, report, filing, creative asset) — not ambient advisory relationships.
5. Sales cycle is days or weeks, not months. Rules out enterprise-tier strategy consulting.

**Wedge pattern:** start as a boutique firm delivering the service the old way. 6 months in, you've quietly automated the repeatable parts. The firm IS the product. You are never "selling software to" incumbents — you are directly replacing them.

**Why this isn't GPT-wrapping:** you're not selling tools to professionals. You're replacing the firm itself. The customer doesn't care what's under the hood — they care that the logo designs arrived in 48 hours and cost $2,000 instead of $25,000.

**Risks:**
- **Brand / trust gap:** clients may not hire a 3-person firm for important work. Mitigation: go downmarket first, build case studies, move upmarket as brand accumulates.
- **Hiring constraint:** you need very senior humans, and senior humans are expensive. Mitigation: equity, project ownership, premium comp funded by the margin expansion.
- **Category selection:** some of the categories above will get eaten by horizontal big-lab products in 1–2 years; most won't. Choose categories where domain nuance, trust relationships, or regulatory wrappers create durability.
- **Scaling curve:** can feel like running a services business (because you are), so it needs to show margin leverage early to read as a venture-scale outcome.

## 3. The Post-App Interface Layer

**Thesis:** Apps exist because databases need UIs. When humans interact with a universal agent via natural language, voice, and camera, the app layer collapses for entire categories. Entire classes of app-native businesses disappear or compress massively — tracking, logging, scheduling, note-taking, journaling, simple databases, todo lists, habit trackers, calorie counters, basic CRM.

**The framing example (Peter Steinberger / OpenClaw):** MyFitnessPal just acquired Cal.ai. On the surface this looks like a sensible tuck-in. In reality both products are about to be redundant. A calorie-tracking app is just a food database + portion math + a UI to log it. When your agent can receive "here's a photo of what I ate," estimate macros, store the entry, and text back end-of-day totals, the app layer has nothing left to sell. The app only ever existed because the database needed a UI. The UI is now natural language.

**Categories that disappear or compress hard:**
- Calorie / workout / habit / sleep trackers
- Simple CRMs and contact managers
- Note-taking and journaling apps
- Task and todo managers
- Budget trackers, expense loggers, personal finance apps
- Basic scheduling and reminder apps
- Weather / news / info-retrieval apps
- Most "utility" apps on mobile
- Language learning apps (for the logging/spaced-repetition piece)
- Basic quantified-self tooling

**Categories that survive:** where the UI *is* the value (games, deeply visual creative tools, live sports), or where proprietary data / network effects / real-time services transcend the interface layer (social, marketplaces with real supply, live entertainment, categories with genuine multi-sided dynamics).

**What gets built in this world:**
- **Protocols and glue infrastructure** that let agents talk to the services that used to be apps. MCP is an early version. There will be more standards, authentication layers, and payment rails.
- **Personal data / context layers** — "my context" stores that agents access on your behalf, portable across providers, owned by the user.
- **Vertical agents** that own a specific job end-to-end (fitness agent, finance agent, logistics agent, travel agent, household agent) in categories that used to be entire app categories.
- **Replacement agents** for category-killer apps, built agent-native from day one, that capture the user relationship the legacy app used to own.
- **Prosumer glue** — a consumer-facing Zapier-for-agents for non-technical operators to wire agents into their own lives.
- **Agent-accessible databases** for verticals where the legacy app's data moat is real but the UI is not (fitness, health, finance records).
- **Output surfaces** — agents still need to *show* things. Ambient displays, voice summaries, on-demand UIs generated for the moment. The "UI as output of computation" pattern is new.

**Open questions:**
- *Why won't incumbents just expose agent APIs?* Because their business model depends on you being in the app, where the ads, upsells, and data capture live. They can't cannibalize voluntarily. This is the classic innovator's-dilemma shape.
- *Why won't Claude / OpenAI build all of this?* They'll build the foundation and the horizontal agents. They won't build every vertical — domain nuance, specialized data, regulated integrations, and category-specific relationships are not lab DNA. Labs optimize for general capability, not vertical care.
- *What's the moat for a vertical agent-native startup?* Three candidates: (1) the vertical's proprietary data, (2) distribution into a specific community, (3) taste — the agent actually does the job well because someone who understands the domain built it.

**Risks:**
- **Timing.** "The app layer dies" could take 2 years or 8. Too early = dead; too late = second mover in a winner-take-most category.
- **Big labs absorbing the horizontal agent layer** and subsuming many verticals by default. Have to pick verticals where labs won't go deep.
- **Consumer WTP.** The incumbent app is often free; convincing users to pay for a "replacement agent" is hard unless you capture genuine new value, not just replicate existing functionality.
- **Distribution.** The incumbent already has millions of users. Agent-native replacements need a sharper reason to switch than "better UX" — usually a new capability or a better business model.
